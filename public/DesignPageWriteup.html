Introduction

The rapid development of biotechnology and recent advancements toward more biocompatible and environmentally friendly solutions for healthcare, scientific and industrial applications trigger the need for more efficient bioprocessing methods, with one of the largest factors involving producing recombinant proteins []. Recombinant genetic materials are transfected into cells using delivery vectors and molecular cloning techniques or placed in a lysate to enable protein production [].

Background/Motivation - Comp

Current methods of CO2 capture

Facing the threat of global warming and extensive environmental destruction, extensive research has been put into identifying new methods of producing clean energy or mitigating the greenhouse gas production from fossil fuels and oil sand operations []. One of the leading sectors is developing technologies for efficient carbon capture and conversion to a more environmentally friendly material. Most of these technologies are chemical based. They often suffer from high building and operational costs, as well as the generation of undesirable toxic substances []. Because of that, there has been a shift towards exploring biological methods such as protein engineering for the use of carbon capture, with the prime candidate being Carbonic Anhydrase (CA).

Background info on CA and how it’s currently being used for carbon capture

CA is a metal binding enzyme responsible for catalysing the conversion of carbon dioxide (CO2) and water (H2O) into bicarbonate (HCO3-) and protons (H+) in physiological systems []. In recent years, with the advancement of bioengineering methods, there has been an increase in the utilization of CA for carbon capture technology [] due to its incredibly high efficiency [].

Lysate Choice

Our first objective is to identify a lysate system capable of supporting the post-translational modifications (PTMs) required for proper folding and assembly of the inactive form of human carbonic anhydrase II alpha (CAIIα). This selected lysate should also provide a meaningful comparison to existing cell-based expression systems while also being suitable for high-throughput protein production.

Although CAIIα is a eukaryotic protein, current cell based protein production systems for carbonic anhydrase utilize E. coli as the host organism due to their high expression level, scalability, and simplicity [].[a] When comparing E. coli lysate to other eukaryotic lysate systems such as wheat germ and rabbit reticulocyte, E. coli lysate system demonstrates superiority.  Wheat germ is not cell-based and therefore fails to provide sufficient basis for evaluation of the p-gel efficiency compared to cell-based systems. On the other hand, rabbit reticulocytes are not readily available and expensive, leading to challenges in the scale up process and resulting in not commonly used for high throughput protein production []. In contrast, one major advantage of using eukaryotic lysate instead of E.coli is the wider range of PTMs that can be facilitated []. However, when looking at CAIIα, the protein only requires _____ for PTMs[b], which can be done by E.coli lysate. Additionally, the issue of incompatibility of using a prokaryotic lysate on a eukaryotic vector can easily be addressed through codon optimization when designing the plasmid.

Due to the reasons mentioned above, our team proceeded with E. coli lysate for the expression of CAIIα using our p-gel system. The E. coli lysate allows for sufficient PTMs while E.coli cells provide a good basis for the comparison and evaluation of the p-gel system efficiency against cell based methods.

Plasmid Design

Our next objective is designing a plasmid that offers high expression levels and strong compatibility with our model protein.

Plasmids are circular, double-stranded DNA molecules and are the most commonly used vectors for protein expression[c] (). They typically carry the gene of interest within a multiple cloning site (MCS), which can be transcribed by the cell’s machinery. Other key elements of a plasmid include:

  • Origin of replication: enables DNA polymerase to replicate the plasmid and increase the gene copy number
  • Promoter:  initiates the transcription of the gene of interest
  • Selection marker: for screening of successfully transfected cells
  • A tag: most often attached to the gene of interest to facilitate downstream protein purification

Aside from the gene inserted at the MCS, other plasmid components are often customizable, allowing researchers to optimize expression levels and ensure compatibility with a broader range of host organisms[d] [].

Our team selected the pET plasmid as our vector. pET plasmids are compatible with bacteria systems such as E.coli with the T7 promoter integrated. T7 promoter utilizes the activity of T7 RNA polymerase that enables quick production of the protein of interest in large quantities[e] []. Additional components in a pET plasmid backbone include a lacI operon, as well as the Rop and Bom sequences. The lacI operon and the Rop, Bom sequences play key roles in regulating the expression of the gene of interest. The plasmid backbone was designed to have the Kanamycin antibiotic resistance (KanR) gene incorporated as the selection marker. P-gel Monomer Design

Our next objective was to computationally determine whether tuning certain qualities of the DNA hydrogel in the system would lead to more effective and efficient protein production.

The efficiency of a DNA hydrogel cell-free system is hypothesized by previous literature[] to be largely due to the following:

  • At a molecular level:
  • The ability to have highly concentrated copies of target gene
  • The surface area of the micropad
  • Prevention of target gene degradation

Due to the highly crosslinked DNA hydrogel, the target gene is concentrated at volumes much higher than if you interspersed normal plasmids or even other cell-free protein systems. It has been observed that the number of target gene copies directly correlated to the amount of protein produced (Park et al., 2009).

The DNA hydrogel extends the lifetime of the cell-free system as it provides protection against target gene degradation- increasing the time of protein expression compared to traditional cell-free systems. To estimate the longevity of the DNA hydrogel, we looked at various aspects of the hydrogel monomer to optimize for stability.

DNA Monomer Conformation

DNA structures with different conformations, such as X-shape, Y-shape, and T-shape, have been used as monomers for the formulation of DNA hydrogels (Um et al., 2006). In the same study, it was identified that the shape of the DNA monomers is a key factor influencing the macroscopic properties of the hydrogel, including swelling degree, tensile strength, and degradation rate. Among the different conformations, crosslinked X-DNAs exhibited the highest swelling degree and tensile strength, as well as the slowest degradation rate.

In a follow-up study, the X-shaped DNA was selected for CFPPS application (Park et al., 2009). However, no justification was provided for this choice.  We aim to re-evaluate the stability of the X, Y, and T-shaped conformations and propose a hypothesis for the selection of X-DNA in the CFPPS application.

The X-DNA is made of 4 strands of DNA, while the Y and T DNA are made of 3 strands of DNA. A schematic of the DNA structures taken from Um et al. (2006) can be seen in  Figure 3.

Hydrogel Dimension + Mold

Finally, in our DNA hydrogel system for cell-free protein synthesis, we aimed to measure and analyze the reproducibility and efficiency of protein production. Thus, to ensure that the same amount of the DNA-hydrogel system is used for each replicate, we developed a polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) mold cast from a 3D-printed resin negative. This standardized mold allows consistent fabrication of P-gel units (from now on referred to as micropads) with a defined geometry, surface area, and volume.

The rationale behind  casting DNA hydrogel into the micropad dimensions stems from findings by Park et al. (2009), who tested a wide variety of dimensions of the hydrogel system and “recognized that the increased surface area of P-gel” had a significant effect on the amount of protein expression.

Thus, these standardized micropads not only ensure experimental consistency in our protein expression experiments, but also allow us to investigate the optimal surface-area-to-volume ratio for protein expression.

“The strong indication of surface area as an important variable in the system could suggest that the bottleneck factor could be due to the RNA Pol’s access to the surface of the gel.”

Materials and Methods:

  • Computer-Aided Design of the resin negative was performed in the web application of Onshape, and exported for 3d printing.
  • the negative mold was printed using [3d printer for resin cite]
  • Casting of the final mold was done with Polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS). PDMS was selected as the mold material due to its favorable mechanochemical properties. PDMS possesses the necessary fluidity to conform to fine features in the resin negative and can be removed easily after curing without excessive mechanical force. Chemically, PDMS is advantageous because of its low surface energy and negative charge, which reduces the risk of adhesion to the DNA hydrogel, itself a negatively charged material. This electrostatic repulsion minimizes tears and deformation during demolding, ensuring the fidelity of the hydrogel shape.

  • The negative mold was printed with Formlabs Clear Resin v4, a photopolymer resin chosen for its compatibility with polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS). This resin exhibited minimal adhesion to PDMS, allowing clean demolding without damage to either material.

Mold design choices:

  • Referring to Figure <>, the spacing between wells (labeled D) was set to 0.05 cm, and the distance between the outermost wells and the mold wall (labeled C) was also set to 0.05 cm. These buffer zones were included to maintain the structural integrity of the PDMS mold during demolding. Since PDMS is pulled out of a rigid resin negative after curing, additional spacing around each well reduces the risk of tearing—especially in the thin regions between wells.

  • Each well dimension was replicated at least 10 times within the mold to ensure sufficient sample numbers for downstream experiments. Different well sizes were incorporated into the same PDMS mold, allowing simultaneous casting of P-gels of different geometries in a single batch. This would improve experimental efficiency and standardization.

  • Referring to the slice view in Figure <>, the height of the resin negative—calculated as dimension A minus dimension C—corresponds to the final height of the PDMS mold. This height was deliberately chosen to ensure the mold would be structurally robust enough to support its own weight during handling and sufficiently thick to allow for clean removal from the resin negative without tearing or deformation.

Well design choices

The final PDMS mold consisted of wells designed to hold the DNA hydrogel during casting; therefore, the dimensions of each well were made equivalent to the intended micropad dimensions.

The well’s height was fixed at 50 mm, based on limitations of the resin 3D printer's z-axis resolution, but the cross-sectional dimensions (width × depth) of each well were varied systematically to investigate how surface area-to-volume ratios affect protein expression efficiency in DNA hydrogels.
