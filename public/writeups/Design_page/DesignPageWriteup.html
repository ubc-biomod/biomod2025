Design

Design Components

To optimize our Cell-Free Protein Production (CFPP) system, we decided to consider 4 factors which contributed to the design (Figure 1).

The four factors are:

  1. Lysate system: The choice of lysate determines whether the gene of interest (GOI) can be effectively expressed.
  2. Plasmid components: The plasmid’s design plays a key role in regulating transcription efficiency, translation rate, detection methods, and downstream isolation processes.
  3. Monomer structure: The shape and composition of the monomer significantly affect the crosslinking process and the stability of the hydrogel.
  4. Hydrogel properties: The shape and dimensions of the hydrogel can influence protein yield, while its diffusive properties have important implications for downstream protein isolation.

Figure 1. The four main design components of our hydrogel system.

Lysate System

Our first objective is to identify a lysate system capable of supporting the post-translational modifications (PTMs) required for proper folding and assembly of the inactive form of human carbonic anhydrase II alpha (CAIIα).

  • Wheat germ expression is not cell-based and therefore fails to provide sufficient basis for evaluation of the p-gel efficiency compared to cell-based systems.
  • Rabbit reticulocytes are not as readily available and expensive,  they have often produced protein yields significantly less than their bacterial counterparts (Gregorio et al., 2019).

One major advantage of using eukaryotic lysate instead of E.coli is the wider range of PTMs that can be facilitated (Gregorio et al. 2019).

However, when looking at CAIIα, the required PTMs as described in Di Fiore et al. and also presented in Table 1 have all been shown to be expressible in previous studies (Tokmakov et al.; Mironova et al.; Zhang et al.). Finally, the issue of incompatibility of using a prokaryotic lysate on a eukaryotic vector can easily be addressed through codon optimization when designing the plasmid.

Plasmid Design

Our next objective is designing a plasmid that offers high expression levels and strong compatibility with our model protein.

Plasmids are circular, double-stranded DNA molecules and are the most commonly used vectors for protein expression (Dewan & Uecker, 2023). The gene of interest is typically inserted within a multiple cloning site (MCS), which can be transcribed by the cell’s machinery. In addition to the gene insertion, many other plasmid components are customizable, allowing researchers to optimize expression levels and ensure compatibility with a broader range of host organisms (Chen et al., 2021).

P-gel Monomer Design

Our next objective was to computationally determine whether we could tune certain qualities of the DNA hydrogel in the system to achieve protein production more effectively and efficiently than cell-based systems.

The efficiency of a DNA hydrogel cell-free system is hypothesized by previous literature (Park et al., 2009)  to be largely due to the following properties:

  • The ability to have highly concentrated copies of target gene
  • Prevention of target gene degradation
  • The surface area of the micropad (p-gel pad)

DNA Monomer Conformation

The shape of the DNA monomers is a key factor influencing the macroscopic properties of the hydrogel, including swelling degree, tensile strength, and degradation rate. Among the different conformations, such as X-shape, Y-shape, and T-shape, have been used as monomers for the formulation of DNA hydrogels (Um et al., 2006).

Figure 3. Schematic of the DNA structures

Taken from Um et al (2006).

In previous literature, the X-shaped DNA was selected for CFPPS application (Park et al., 2009). However, no justification was provided for this choice so we re-evaluated the stability of the X, Y, and T-shaped conformations to select a final monomer conformation for our CFPPS application.

After conducting structural stability assessments using stability and modelling software, we found that the most stable conformation was the X-DNA formation, coming in with a measured RMSD of 1.13 and an secondary structural free energy of -127.30 kcal/mol.

X-DNA Arm Sequences

Having decided on an optimal monomer structure, we then decide to further optimize its stability by adjusting additional properties of the X-DNA arm sequences.

Since our overarching goal was to prevent degradation of the target CAIIα gene within the hydrogel, we considered two properties that would influence both monomer and hydrogel stability- X-DNA arm length and X-DNA GC content (%).

The specific methods and scripts for all the simulations conducted below can be found in the Simulations tab.

Sequence arm length

Longer arm sequences increase the average size of pores within the hydrogel, thereby loosening the hydrogel and making the final assembled structure more fluid. This is especially influential, as previous experimental literature noted that when the stiffness of the gel decreases expression tends to increase (Park et al., 2008). Additionally, through our simulations, we observed that longer arms also tend to have a lower structural Gibbs free energy, which indicates they are more stable.

However, longer X-DNA strands may also introduce additional problems. As seen in figure 4, having too long of an arm increases the chance of proteins entering the hydrogel system. This is an issue because Park et al (2009) suggested that most of the expression occurred at the surface of the gel. For this reason, we concluded that a strand length of 40-50 bp provides small enough pores while maintaining relatively low Gibbs free energy.

Sequence GC Content (%)

Due to the additional hydrogen bond in G–C pairs, having more GC base pairs gives the double strands of the DNA duplex stronger base stacking interactions, which in turn prevents DNA monomer denaturation and degradation, especially surrounding the target genes.

Decreased Gibbs free energy indicates an improved resistance to thermal fluctuations and also promotes stronger and more persistent crosslinking between nanostars. This suggests that having a higher GC content could otherwise lead to denaturation or degradation. If the p-gel is not denatured, it could even be extracted after initial expression and placed in a new lysate system to re-express and reused.

After all our simulation evaluations, the final design for our monomer crosslinker is an X shaped DNA molecule with a GC content of approximately 60-70%.

Hydrogel Dimension + Mold

Finally, we aimed to measure and analyze the reproducibility and efficiency of protein production.

To ensure that the same amount of the DNA-hydrogel system is used for each replicate, we developed a polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) mold cast from a 3D-printed resin negative. This standardized mold allows consistent fabrication of P-gel units (from now on referred to as micropads) with a defined geometry, surface area, and volume.

The rationale behind  casting DNA hydrogel into the micropad dimensions stems from findings by Park et al. (2009), who tested a wide variety of dimensions of the hydrogel system and “recognized that the increased surface area of P-gel” had a significant effect on the amount of protein expression. We planned to cast our hydrogels within these molds to also investigate the optimal surface-area-to-volume ratio for protein expression.

Materials and Methods:

  • Computer-Aided Design of the resin negative was performed in the web application of Onshape.

Well design choices

The final PDMS mold consisted of wells designed to hold the DNA hydrogel during casting; therefore, the dimensions of each well were made equivalent to the intended micropad dimensions.

The well’s height was fixed at 50 mm, based on limitations of the resin 3D printer's z-axis resolution, but the cross-sectional dimensions (width × depth) of each well were varied systematically to investigate how surface area-to-volume ratios affect protein expression efficiency in DNA hydrogels.
