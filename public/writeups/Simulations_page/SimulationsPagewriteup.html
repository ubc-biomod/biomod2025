1. Introduction

Over the past decades, DNA has been used as a fundamental building block for synthesizing nanostructures with precisely controlled physicochemical properties for a wide range of biomedical applications [1]. In the early stages, the absence of computational design tools limited DNA nanostructure research to relatively simple, single-stranded constructs [2]. Recent advances in computer-aided design (CAD) software and increased computational power have enabled the creation of far more complex architectures, driving rapid growth in this field.

Several open-source software tools have been developed to streamline the DNA nanostructure design process and improve the stability and quality of the final constructs. One example is oxView, a web-based application that enables researchers to build and edit custom 3D shapes of DNA nanostructures [3]. oxView supports both lattice-based designs and free-form geometries with complex shapes. It also provides features such as customizable strand color palettes, the ability to merge structures created in different design tools, interrogation of individual atoms, and bulk editing capabilities [3].

Designs created in oxView can be exported to oxDNA, a coarse-grained molecular dynamics (MD) simulator that tracks the positions and forces acting on clusters of atoms within the structure [4]. oxDNA updates particle motion using either Langevin or Brownian dynamics, depending on the desired physical modeling conditions. Simulation outputs include metrics such as the root mean square deviation (RMSD) of atomic positions, which quantifies the average deviation between molecular conformations over time [5]. A high RMSD indicates greater structural fluctuations and may suggest reduced stability. oxDNA can also compute the Gibbs free energy along the simulation trajectory, where lower, or more negative, values are generally associated with more thermodynamically stable structures [6].

While oxView and oxDNA focus primarily on design and stability analysis, other software tools such as NUPACK are designed to analyze the equilibrium base-pairing properties of specific DNA nanostructure assemblies [7]. In other words, NUPACK can be used to evaluate the probability that a given set of DNA sequences will self-assemble into the desired shape. The software can also estimate the free energy associated with the predicted structure, providing additional information into its stability.

Despite the availability of powerful CAD software for sequence design, structural modeling, and stability analysis, there is currently no software capable of directly evaluating macroscopic properties of DNA nanostructures, such as tensile strength or diffusive behavior. Instead, customized computational workflows using programming languages such as MATLAB, Python, or C++ are used to fill this gap due to their extensive scientific libraries for numerical analysis, statistical modeling, and data visualization.

  1. Background/Motivation

The computational simulation resources described above were utilized to achieve three goals:

  • First, to identify the most stable monomer, to serve as a building block for our hydrogel, among the 3 potential shapes: X, Y, and T.
  • Second, to optimize the selected monomer for maximum stability and the highest likelihood of correct self-assembly.
  • Finally, to investigate the macroscopic properties of the DNA hydrogel, specifically examining the accumulation of the produced carbonic anhydrase (CA) within the network.

  1. Method

  1. Monomer Conformation

Stability assessments on different monomer designs were evaluated using the molecular dynamics (MD) software oxDNA, based on Langevin dynamics principles. MD simulations predict the motion of each atom over time, allowing us to track the trajectory of the molecule and compute the associated Gibbs free energy and Root Mean Square Deviation (RMSD) . These metrics were then used to assess and compare the structural stability of the DNA monomers.

Gibbs free energy represents the energy available for a system to do work. At chemical equilibrium, a negative Gibbs free energy indicates that a reaction is spontaneous []. By measuring the Gibbs free energy of the three DNA monomers, we can assess their ability to spontaneously form the desired shapes. RMSD, on the other hand, quantifies how much a molecule's trajectory deviates from a reference conformation. Typically, this is the conformation with the lowest Gibbs free energy. This reference conformation is obtained by performing an initial Monte Carlo simulation to “relax” the DNA structures in oxView.

  1. P-gel arm GC + Length

Our workflow was separated into two main processes: re-assessing the stability of DNA monomers with different geometry, and optimizing the stability of the monomers by adjusting the number of base pairs in the monomer’s arms and the GC content of each strand of the monomer.

Assessing Stability:

We first constructed 3 DNA structures: X-shaped monomer, Y-shaped monomer, and T-shaped monomer using oxView. The structures were relaxed into the conformations with lowest Gibbs free energy level. The relaxed structures were then imputed into oxDNA where molecular dynamics simulations using a Langevin thermostat were carried out. The trajectory of the molecule was recorded, and was analysed for the average level of Gibbs free energy and RMSD. The structure with the lowest Gibbs free energy across the trajectory and the lowest RMSD is considered to be the most stable and selected for further optimization.

Optimization:

The goal of the optimization process was to improve the structural stability of our monomers by varying two physical parameters: the number of base pairs per arm and the GC content. We first developed a Python script to generate 10 unique sequences for each experimental condition, varying arm length and GC content. These sequences were then analyzed using NUPACK, a software tool for modeling nucleic acid thermodynamics. For each monomer, we evaluated Gibbs free energy and the probability of correct base pairing. Monomers with lower Gibbs free energy and higher pairing probabilities were considered more stable. To assess the influence of each parameter on stability, we plotted the average Gibbs free energy across different arm lengths and GC contents.   1. Diffusion

The hydrogel will be submerged in E. coli lysate to facilitate protein production. Given the hydrogel’s porosity and the concentration gradient between the E. coli cellular machinery and the produced CA in the lysate versus within the hydrogel, both components may diffuse into and accumulate within the hydrogel mesh. Such accumulation may reduce production efficiency. In this study, we aim to evaluate the diffusive properties of CA, holoenzyme RNA polymerase, ribosomes, and CA mRNA to determine whether significant aggregation occurs within the hydrogel mesh.

To begin the process, we first assessed whether these biological molecules could diffuse into the hydrogel mesh based on their sizes. Using the number of 20 base pairs per arm for the X-DNA, the mesh size was calculated to be 13.6 nm [8, p.177]. The size of human CA II mRNA, determined from whole genome sequencing, was found to be 15.16 µm [9, 10]. On the other hand, E. coli RNA polymerase has a molecular weight of 449 kDa [11], which corresponds to an approximate diameter of 12.98 nm [12]. The E. coli ribosome has a diameter of 26 nm [13], and the size of human CA II protein is approximately 2.01 nm [14].

From the size of the molecules, it is apparent that only RNA polymerase and CA II are small enough to potentially diffuse into the hydrogel mesh. However, experimental measurements indicate that the actual mesh size of X-DNA hydrogels is closer to 12.3 ± 1.4 nm [15]. Since larger molecules have a lower probability of diffusing through the mesh, and the size of E. coli RNA polymerase is very close to the upper limit of this measured range [16], its diffusion into the network is likely minimal and can be considered negligible. Moreover, once RNA polymerase produces the mRNA templates for protein synthesis, these mRNA molecules can be reused multiple times in the lysate. Therefore, even if the concentration of RNA polymerase in the lysate decreases, the overall protein yield would not be significantly affected.

In contrast, due to its much smaller size, CA II can readily pass through the mesh and diffuse into the DNA polymer network. If CA II were to accumulate within the hydrogel, it could lower the yield in the lysate solution. To evaluate whether it would be beneficial for us to extract CA II from the gel, we modeled the diffusion of the protein into the polymer network by numerically solving the Fickian diffusion equation.

Given that CA is not significantly smaller than the mesh size, its diffusion is largely influenced by the solution-mediated transport through the mesh, with the effective diffusion coefficient primarily determined by obstruction effects.

There are several important polymer properties that affect the ability of a solute to diffuse through the hydrogel mesh. The first is junction functionality, which describes the number of chains converging at each junction point. By taking the center of the X-DNA as the junction point, our p-gel has a junction functionality of 4. The degree of polymerization between junctions is defined as the number of repeating units between each junction. In our system, each sugar phosphate backbone in DNA is treated as a unit. Once the X-DNA strands assemble into a mesh, there are 40 base pairs between junctions, giving a degree of polymerization of 40. Since each nucleotide in DNA is linked to its neighbors through two phosphodiester bonds, the number of backbone bonds per unit is 2.

Based on values reported by Um et al., hydrogels made with X-DNA monomers exhibit a monomer incorporation efficiency of approximately 83.5 ± 2.1%, corresponding to a swollen polymer volume fraction of 0.835. For the solute, the hydrodynamic radius is taken as the Stokes radius of carbonic anhydrase, 2.01nm [17]. The polymer fibers, represented by DNA, have a radius of 1nm [18]. The average bond length of the polymeric repeating unit in the hydrogel mesh was calculated by summing the average bond lengths for all atoms in a nucleotide and dividing by four, yielding a value of 0.137 nm [19].

The persistence length of DNA is approximately 50 nm, while the effective bond length can be approximated as the distance between nucleotides, 0.34 nm [20, 21]. Using these parameters, the effective diffusion coefficient of carbonic anhydrase through the DNA hydrogel mesh was calculated to be 103 μm²/s.

To validate these results, we compared our free diffusion (D₀) value and calculated mesh size with values reported in the literature. The mesh size obtained from the transport calculator was 12.9 nm, which falls within the range reported by Park et al. (12.3 ± 1.4 nm). Additionally, the free diffusion coefficient of carbonic anhydrase β reported in the literature is 107 μm²/s. This is slightly lower than the D₀ value of 127 μm²/s predicted by mesh transport theory. The discrepancy may be attributed to structural differences, as the β isoform is composed of two subunits and is therefore larger than the α isoform. Since the α isoform is smaller, it can move through the hydrogel mesh more easily, resulting in a higher diffusion coefficient.

For the temporal dimension, the system was integrated using the classical fourth-order Runge-Kutta (RK4) method. Compared to lower order RK, the fourth order approximation is closer to the actual value. RK4 estimates the slope of the solution of the differential equation four times with the next slope being the estimate of the value of the function midway through the interval. This process is carried out for each time step and combined into a weighted average, effectively canceling error terms up to order h⁴, similar to a fourth order Taylor Expansion. This yields a local truncation error of O(h⁵) and a global error of O(h⁴), providing significantly higher accuracy and stability than Euler or other lower-order methods for non-stiff problems.
