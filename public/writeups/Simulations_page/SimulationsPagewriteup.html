1. Modelling Software and Evaluation Metrics

Over the past decades, DNA has been used as a fundamental building block for synthesizing nanostructures with precisely controlled physicochemical properties for a wide range of biomedical applications (Ke et al., 2018). In the early stages, the absence of computational design tools limited DNA nanostructure research to relatively simple, single-stranded constructs (Tian et al, 2022)  but recent advances in computer-aided design (CAD) software and increased computational power have enabled the creation of far more complex architectures, driving rapid growth in this field.

For our project, we took advantage of the various CAD softwares as described below:

  1. oxView

oxView is a web-based application that enables researchers to build and edit custom 3D shapes of DNA nanostructures (Bohlin et al., 2022). oxView supports both lattice-based designs and free-form geometries with complex shapes. It also provides features such as customizable strand color palettes, the ability to merge structures created in different design tools, interrogation of individual atoms, and bulk editing capabilities (Bohlin et al., 2022).

  1. oxDNA

Designs created in oxView can be exported to oxDNA, a coarse-grained molecular dynamics (MD) simulator that tracks the positions and forces acting on clusters of atoms within the structure (Doye et al., 2023). oxDNA updates particle motion using either Langevin or Brownian dynamics, depending on the desired physical modeling conditions. Simulation outputs include metrics such as the residue mean square deviation (RMSD) of atomic positions, which quantifies the average deviation between molecular conformations over time compared to a reference state. A high RMSD indicates greater structural fluctuations and may suggest lower stability. oxDNA can also compute the Gibbs free energy along the simulation trajectory, where lower, or more negative, values are generally associated with more thermodynamically stable structures.

  1. NUPACK

While oxView and oxDNA focus primarily on stability analysis, other software tools such as NUPACK are designed to analyze the equilibrium base-pairing properties of specific DNA nanostructure assemblies. In other words, NUPACK can be used to evaluate the probability that a given set of DNA sequences will self-assemble into the desired shape. The software can also estimate the free energy associated with the predicted structure, providing additional information into its stability.

Gibbs free energy represents the energy available for a system to do work. At chemical equilibrium, a negative Gibbs free energy indicates that a reaction is spontaneous. By measuring the Gibbs free energy of the three DNA monomers, we can assess their ability to spontaneously form the desired shapes. RMSD, on the other hand, quantifies how much a molecule's trajectory deviates from a reference conformation. Typically, this is the conformation with the lowest Gibbs free energy. This reference conformation is obtained by performing an initial Monte Carlo simulation to “relax” the DNA structures in oxView.

Despite the availability of powerful CAD software for sequence design, structural modeling, and stability analysis, there is currently no software capable of directly evaluating macroscopic properties of DNA nanostructures, such as tensile strength or diffusive behavior. Instead, customized computational workflows using python to fill this gap due to their extensive scientific libraries for numerical analysis, statistical modeling, and data visualization.

  1. Motivation

The computational simulation resources described above were utilized to achieve three goals:

  • First, to identify the most stable monomer, to serve as a building block for our hydrogel, among the 3 potential shapes: X, Y, and T.
  • Second, to optimize the selected monomer for maximum stability and the highest likelihood of correct self-assembly.
  • Finally, to investigate the macroscopic properties of the DNA hydrogel, specifically examining the accumulation of the produced carbonic anhydrase (CA) within the network.

  1. Methods

  1. Monomer Conformation

  • oxView: Constructed 3D models of the X, Y, and T-shaped DNA monomers.  Within oxView, a Monte Carlo simulation was then performed on these models to identify the most energetically favorable conformations, a process referred to as monomer relaxation.
  • oxDNA: Used to conduct stability assessments on the previously relaxed monomer designs.
  • NUPACK: simulated the monomer’s folding and ligation under experimental thermal conditions, as well as define free energy of overall structure

From our simulations, we observed the X-DNA has the lowest Gibbs free energy, indicating it is the most thermodynamically stable and most likely to assemble into the desired structure during a one-pot reaction. The RMSD trajectories also show that the X-DNA exhibited the least deviation from its relaxed conformation in comparison to the others.   1. P-gel arm GC + Length

Since we validate the hypothesis that the X-DNA is the most stable and promising candidate among the monomers hence we chose to proceed with further optimization exclusively for the X-DNA.

We focused on two main characteristics of the monomer:

  1. The length of the X-DNA arms
  2. The GC content of the X-DNA arms

Length Optimization

The length of the monomer arm was evaluated based on the number of base pairs (bp) it was composed of, including the overhang. We tested five conditions: 20 bp, 30bp, 40 bp, 50 bp, and 60 bp. To ensure statistical significance, for each target sequence length, we generated and tested 10 different sequences. The spontaneous formation of the monomer from these sequences were simulated using NUPACK, where the molecules’ Gibbs free energy values were recorded. The graph in Figure 1 shows a linear downward trend, and we observe that shorter monomers have higher Gibbs free energy, and are therefore less stable.

Additionally, it should be noted that, compared to monomers with longer arms, those with shorter arms behave more like rigid rods than flexible ropes. Park et al. (2009) speculated that the increased rigidity of these monomers could stiffen the gel and ultimately lead to a decrease in overall protein yield. However, a major challenge associated with increasing the arm length of the monomer lies in maintaining an appropriate hydrogel mesh size. The mesh must remain relatively small to prevent ribosomes, DNA polymerase, and other cellular machinery from diffusing into the gel and becoming trapped within it. Having a small mesh size ensures that all protein production occurs within the lysate rather than inside the gel.

GC Content Optimization

Similar to the arm length optimization, we generated at least 10 unique X-DNA sequences for each of the following 5 different GC content levels: 40%, 50%, 60%, 70%, and 80%. Unique X-DNA arm sequences were generated via Python script(s) developed by our team.These sequences were subsequently analyzed using NUPACK to assess the overall Gibbs free energy of each monomer. The graph in Figure 2, which shows the average free energy for each GC content condition, indicates that monomers with higher GC content have lower Gibbs free energy and are therefore more stable. However, monomers with higher GC content are also more prone to forming intramolecular bonds and undesirable internal loop structures, which may hinder the self-assembly process (Liu et al., 2014). We can observe that there is indeed a gradual slowing of the average free energy decrease as you continue to increase the GC content (%). Therefore, we chose a GC content of approximately 60-70% for our X-DNA monomer to maximize stability but still ensure that relatively no internal loops were forming.

  1. Diffusion

The hydrogel will be submerged in E. coli lysate to start the protein production process. Given the hydrogel’s porosity and the concentration gradient between the E. coli cellular machinery and the produced CA in the lysate versus within the hydrogel, both components may diffuse into and accumulate within the hydrogel mesh. Such accumulation may reduce production efficiency. In this study, we aim to evaluate the diffusive properties of CA, holoenzyme RNA polymerase, ribosomes, and CA mRNA to determine whether significant aggregation occurs within the hydrogel mesh.

To begin the process, we first assessed whether these biological molecules could diffuse into the hydrogel mesh based on their sizes. Using the number of 20 base pairs per arm for the X-DNA, the mesh size was calculated to be 13.6 nm (Vesely, 2004b). The size of human CA II mRNA, determined from whole genome sequencing, was found to be 15.16 µm (Roth et al. 2018, CA2 Carbonic Anhydrase 2 [Homo Sapiens (Human)] - Gene - NCBI, n.d.). On the other hand, E. coli RNA polymerase has a molecular weight of 449 kDa (Finn, 2000), which corresponds to an approximate diameter of 12.98 nm (Fluidic Sciences, 2025). The E. coli ribosome has a diameter of 26 nm, and the size of human CA II protein is approximately 2.01 nm (Milo et al. Nucl. Acids Res, 2010).

From the size of the molecules, it is apparent that only RNA polymerase and CA II are small enough to potentially diffuse into the hydrogel mesh. However, experimental measurements indicate that the actual mesh size of X-DNA hydrogels is closer to 12.3 ± 1.4 nm (Um et al., 2006). Since larger molecules have a lower probability of diffusing through the mesh, and the size of E. coli RNA polymerase is very close to the upper limit of this measured range (Axpe et al., 2019), its diffusion into the network is likely minimal and can be considered negligible. Moreover, once RNA polymerase produces the mRNA templates for protein synthesis, the mRNA can be reused multiple times in the lysate. Therefore, even if the concentration of RNA polymerase in the lysate decreases, the overall protein yield would not be significantly affected.

In contrast, due to its much smaller size, CA II can readily pass through the mesh and diffuse into the DNA polymer network. If CA II were to accumulate within the hydrogel, it could lower the yield in the lysate solution. To evaluate whether it would be beneficial for us to extract CA II from the gel, we modeled the diffusion of the protein into the polymer network by numerically solving the Fickian diffusion equation.

System and Constraints

The p-gel is a rectangular prism, so CA can diffuse into the hydrogel through all six faces. Because there are only three unique face types, we model transport across those three faces and invoke symmetry by multiplying each contribution by two to obtain the total influx across each axis.

Given the very high solubility of CA (Fisher et al., 2012) and the long reaction time, we assume the lysate becomes well mixed. Thus, the CA concentration in the bulk lysate and at the hydrogel–lysate interface is spatially uniform. In addition, because CA has a long half-life (Ye & Lu, 2014) relative to the experiment duration, we neglect enzymatic degradation over the entire run.

Given that CA is not significantly smaller than the mesh size, its diffusion is largely influenced by the solution-mediated transport through the mesh, with the effective diffusion coefficient primarily determined by obstruction effects.

There are several important polymer properties that affect the ability of a solute to diffuse through the hydrogel mesh. The first is junction functionality, which describes the number of chains converging at each junction point. By taking the center of the X-DNA as the junction point, our p-gel has a junction functionality of 4. The degree of polymerization between junctions is defined as the number of repeating units between each junction. In our system, each sugar phosphate backbone in DNA is treated as a unit. Once the X-DNA strands assemble into a mesh, there are 40 base pairs between junctions, giving a degree of polymerization of 40. Since each nucleotide in DNA is linked to its neighbors through two phosphodiester bonds, the number of backbone bonds per unit is 2.

Based on values reported by Um et al., 2006 hydrogels made with X-DNA monomers exhibit a monomer incorporation efficiency of approximately 83.5 ± 2.1%, corresponding to a swollen polymer volume fraction of 0.835. For the solute, the hydrodynamic radius is taken as the Stokes radius of carbonic anhydrase, 2.01nm (Milo et al., 2009). The polymer fibers, represented by DNA, have a radius of 1nm (Milo et al., 2009). The average bond length of the polymeric repeating unit in the hydrogel mesh was calculated by summing the average bond lengths for all atoms in a nucleotide and dividing by four, yielding a value of 0.137 nm (Heyrovska, 2008).

The persistence length of DNA is approximately 50 nm, while the effective bond length can be approximated as the distance between nucleotides, 0.34 nm (Milo et al., 2009b, Wang, 2021). Using these parameters, the effective diffusion coefficient of carbonic anhydrase through the DNA hydrogel mesh was calculated to be 103 μm²/s.

To validate these results, we compared our free diffusion (D₀) value and calculated mesh size with values reported in the literature. The mesh size obtained from the transport calculator was 12.9 nm, which falls within the range reported by Park et al. (12.3 ± 1.4 nm). Additionally, the free diffusion coefficient of carbonic anhydrase β reported in the literature is 107 μm²/s (Milo et al., 2009b). This is slightly lower than the D₀ value of 127 μm²/s predicted by mesh transport theory. The discrepancy may be attributed to structural differences, as the β isoform is composed of two subunits and is therefore larger than the α isoform. Since the α isoform is smaller, it can move through the hydrogel mesh more easily, resulting in a higher diffusion coefficient.

For the temporal integration, the system was solved using the implicit multistep variable-order (BDF) method via Python’s solve_ivp module. Because the system spans a large time frame, the PDE is extremely stiff. Explicit methods, such as Euler’s method or the Runge-Kutta methods (RK23, RK45, DOP853), result in divergence and numerical instability. The BDF method was chosen because it provides enhanced stability for stiff problems while maintaining a relatively low computational cost. This implicit approach approximates the derivative at the current time step by using a backward extrapolation from previous time points, allowing larger time steps without losing stability.
