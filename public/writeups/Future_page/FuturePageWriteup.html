Future

Future Experiments

While we have successfully expressed human carbonic anhydrase II alpha (CAIIα) using multiple platforms—including bacterial expression systems, protein-producing DNA hydrogels (p-gels), and standard plasmid-based cell-free systems—we acknowledge that our work is still in progress. Several important experiments remain to be completed in order to fully validate and optimize our production and application pipelines. These include, but are not limited to:

  1. Repeat the p-gel micropad experiment while removing p-gel before protein isolation
  2. Run replicates for unique micropad dimensions to optimize for surface area
  3. Repeat validation assays to confirm results and optimize conditions

For many of our experiments, due to time constraints, we were unable to rerun experiments a sufficient number of times to optimize conditions fully. As an extension in the future, we would like to repeat the synthesis of the p-gel to test various conditions and times. This future research would enable us to determine the most robust methodology for producing a stable p-gel efficiently, in terms of both cost and time. Once conditions have been optimized, we would like to produce replicates to test protein production rates with different surface areas to validate our computational findings. As well, future experiments should include repeated iterations of the final protein validation assays. This would include repeating our CA activity assay and BCA assay to confirm the accuracy of our results more rigorously. The SDS-PAGE also requires optimization of loading conditions and sample preparation in future iterations of our experiments, as we saw inconclusive results from our initial trial.

Additionally, we hope to collaborate with other project teams and labs within our UBC campus and beyond to provide them with our produced carbonic anhydrase for investigation. By partnering with other groups, we aim to explore how sustainable our expressed CAIIα is within real-world applications and other possible uses of a cell-free protein production system to synthesize functional proteins like CA. These collaborations could provide valuable insights into both the utility and limitations of our current production platforms. We believe that these collaborations would also allow for increased dialogue and could help raise awareness on innovative solutions to current pressing climate concerns.

Cost Analysis of Protein Production:

Comparative Overview of Hydrogel-Based Cell-Free Systems

Why Is This Important?

The cost of protein production directly impacts protein research, product development, and scalability. Bacterial protein production can be extremely expensive, so in recent years, cell-free protein synthesis methods have been proposed as an efficient and rapid alternative that bypasses many bottlenecks and barriers when compared to cell-based methods (Lee and Kim, 2018). However, we would like to make a more formal investigation of the two methods at various scales.

Our Models:

In order to understand the economic and cost tradeoffs for cell-free protein production (CFPP) in comparison to cell-based protein production (CBPP), we chose to investigate three common use cases of protein production.

Cost over time for a constant production cycle favors cell-based protein expression systems in all models.

Thus, it required significantly higher scales of equipment to process the volume. However, the nature of CFPP systems means that much of the raw material costs are higher ,as previously mentioned in literature (Thaoe et al., 2020).

  •  Cost Efficiency (mg per $)

This metric tells us how much protein we get per unit of cost. It allows us to see whether the protein becomes cheaper to produce per cycle.

Since our final calculations for the hydrogel-based CFPP system had a per-cycle cost that was significantly higher than not only the cell-based per-cycle cost, but also significantly higher than the respective cell-free initial cost, the cost efficiency for CFPP never lowered. On the other hand, as expected of cell-based systems, the costs per cycle are much lower because the bacteria are much more self-sustaining than cell-free systems, and thus the cost efficiency lowers to a consistent price per mg.

Final Discussion

  • Raw Materials Costs: Previous literature has indicated that raw material costs take up the majority of cell-free expression systems (Thaore et al., 2020). A similar pattern exists for our hydrogel-based CFPP system, as raw materials need to be purchased fresh for each new batch of expression, and cannot be regenerated like the cell-based expression. This includes all components of the p-gel and processing the DNA hydrogel to form the micropads.

Our CFPP cost breakdown shows that lysate has taken up the majority of costs, and that lysate is required to be purchased for each new batch of expression.

  1. Figure 3. Cell-based System and Hydrogel Cell-Free System Cost Breakdown

  • Recycling DNA (micropads)

Recycling DNA does reduce per cycle costs ( Supplementary Figures 1a, Figure 2a and 3a) but not significantly, as DNA is not the primary contributor to the cost.

  • Switching Proteins

Previous literature has suggested that a significant use case for cell-free production systems is for the large majority of orphan drugs and single-use protein production required for certain projects (Dondapati et al., 2020).

With the hydrogel-based cell-free systems, the only change is a plasmid template switch, which does not change the costs.

With cell-based systems, it requires a recombinant transformation, which in our models we had set a generous 24 additional hours.

Additionally, when prototyping proteins, stable cell-line production costs and GMP certification must be considered. For protein expressed via bacterial recombinants to be used in clinical settings, stable cell lines must be developed and certified. This often takes 12-16 months, in addition to the annual certification renewal (est. $46000 annual cost for small-scale biotechs). This was a time cost that was not factored into our analysis since the 12-16 months would skew production rates to make our models incomparable with each other. This time and resource cost significantly hinders speed-to-market, which our hydrogel-based CFPP system does not face, as it purchases the valid DNA template each time.

Conclusion

In the final conclusion, we note that cell-free systems are not suited for long-term expressions of one consistent protein due to the high raw material cost per cycle. However, there is a market for orphan drugs and prototype protein amounts, especially if used for prototyping in clinical settings. Production here is bottlenecked by 12-16 months of waiting due to stable cell line development. This is a constraint that CFPP can easily overcome, and further investigation should focus on these orphan drugs  or functional screening for future applications of CFPP systems.
