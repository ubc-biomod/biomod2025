Cost Analysis of Protein Production: Comparative Overview of Hydrogel-Based Cell Free Systems

Why Is This Important?

The cost of protein production directly impacts how protein research, product development, and scalability. Bacterial protein production can be extremely expensive, so in recent years, cell-free protein synthesis methods have been proposed as an efficient and rapid alternative that bypasses many bottlenecks and barriers when compared to cell-based methods (Lee and Kim, 2018). However, we would like to make a more formal investigation between the two methods at various scales.

Our Models:

In order to understand the economic and cost tradeoffs for cell-free protein production (CFPP) in comparison to cell-based protein production (CBPP), we chose to investigate three common use cases of protein production.

We do note that Cell-based E.Coli can reach up to expression rates of up to even  10mg/mL (Kleiner-Grote et al., 2018), however, usually those optimizations are extremely protein-dependent and not intended for arbitrary protein. Additionally, to reach these metrics, significant time, resources and research is required into investigating the scale-up for small-scale and large-scale for both cell-based systems and cell-free systems. Without the actual experimental data and knowledge of real-world constraints, we’ve made the assumption these production rates for both CFPP and CBPP are able to scale uniformly to different scales.

Given that there is a higher production rate in the cell-free system, from a purely production rate standpoint, CFPP has an upper hand in faster turnaround for experiments, product development, and urgent manufacturing scenarios—such as pandemic response or point-of-care diagnostics.

  •  Cost Efficiency (mg per $)

This metric tells us how much protein we get per unit of cost. It allows us to see whether the protein becomes cheaper to produce per cycle.

Since our final calculations for the hydrogel-based CFPP system had a per-cycle cost that was significantly higher than not only the cell-based per-cycle cost, but also significantly higher than the respective cell-free initial cost, the cost efficiency for CFPP never lowered. On the other hand, as expected of cell-based systems, the costs per cycle are much lower because the bacteria is much more self-sustaining than cell-free systems, and thus the cost efficiency lowers to a consistent price per mg.

Final Discussion

  • Raw Materials Costs: Previous literature has indicated that raw material costs take up the majority of cell-free expression systems (Thaore et al., 2020). A similar pattern exists for our hydrogel-based CFPP system, as raw materials need to be purchased fresh for each new batch of expression, and cannot be regenerated like the cell-based expression. This includes all components of the p-gel and processing the DNA hydrogel to form the micropads.

Our CFPP cost breakdown shows that lysate has taken up the majority of costs, and that lysate is required to be purchased for each new batch of expression.

Figure 3. Hydrogel Cell-Free Cost breakdown

  • Recycling DNA (micropads)

Recycling DNA does reduce per cycle costs ( Supplementary Figures 1a, Figure 2a and 3a) but not significantly, as DNA is not the primary contributor to the cost.

  • Switching Proteins

Previous literature has suggested that a significant use case for cell-free production systems are for the large majority of orphan drugs and single use protein production required for certain projects (Dondapati et al., 2020).

With the hydrogel-based cell-free systems: the only change is a plasmid template switch, which does not change the costs.

With cell-based systems, it requires a recombinant transformation, which in our models we had set a generous 24 additional hours.

Additionally, a cost we did not consider for this use case is the requirement for stable cell-line production for each new fresh protein. For protein expressed via bacterial recombinants to be used in clinical settings, stable cell lines must be developed and certified. This often takes 12-16 months, in addition to the annual certification renewment (est $46000 annual cost for small-scale biotechs). This was time cost and resource cost that was not factored into our analysis since the 12-16 months would skew production rates to make our models incomparable with each other. This time and resource cost does significantly hinder speed-to-market, which our hydrogel-based CFPP system does not face, as it purchases the valid dna template each time.

Conclusion

In the final conclusion, we note that cell-free systems are not suited for long term expressions of one consistent protein due to the high raw material cost per cycle. However, there is a market for orphan drugs and prototype protein amounts, especially if used for prototyping in clinical settings, production here is bottle-necked by 12-16 months of waiting due to stable cell line development. This is a constraint that CFPP can easily overcome, and that further investigation should focus on these orphan or detachable prototype proteins for applications of CFPP systems.
