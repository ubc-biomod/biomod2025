Project Background

Carbonic anhydrases (CAs) are zinc-complex enzymes that catalyze the conversion of carbon dioxide (CO2) into bicarbonate and hydrogen ions. Its ability to convert CO2 into other molecules has motivated recent research to investigate its applications in reducing elevated atmospheric CO2 concentrations (Chegwidden and Carter, 2000). However, existing CA-based carbon capture systems are limited in their scalability for CA production. For instance, E. coli transformation-based CA production is limited in its production rate and requires additional energy input to maintain cell viability.

Typically, in a cellular system, such as in bacterial cells, proteins are produced in a process involving the transcription of a gene into an RNA intermediate, followed by translation into a protein product. However, limitations arise in maintaining large-scale cell-based platforms for protein production in terms of maintaining cell cultures and the scalability of the system (Cid and Jorge, 2021). An alternative solution that has been gaining traction in recent literature is the development of cell-free protein production (CFPP) systems, due to their greater diversity of amenable proteins and ability to easily modify synthesis components[a] (Zemella et al.).

Hydrogels are three-dimensional structural networks of polymers that can absorb large amounts of fluids (Ho et al., 2022). DNA hydrogels are composed of cross-linked DNA strands, which can be functionalized to include protein-encoding genes within their scaffolds (Wu et al., 2024). When supplemented with cellular lysates containing additional factors necessary for protein expression, DNA hydrogels are capable of synthesizing large amounts of the desired protein product in a cell-free manner. Recent literature has demonstrated this concept by incorporating these DNA-based hydrogels into existing CFPP methods, reporting greater protein production rates and yields compared to current in vitro methods (Park et al., 2009). Yet, existing works only demonstrate the system’s capabilities using fluorescent proteins as a proof-of-concept experiment (Park et al., 2009).

With rising concerns about climate change, developing solutions to address issues such as atmospheric CO2 levels becomes increasingly critical. Studying possible biochemical solutions using enzymes, such as CA, that could help address rising CO2  concentrations may be the key to mitigating current environmental challenges. Integrating novel DNA protein-producing hydrogel technology in a feasible project for undergraduate researchers, we aim to demonstrate the abilities of DNA hydrogel-based CFPP to produce carbonic anhydrase (CA), with the goal of employing its carbon capture capabilities to aid in the fight against climate change.

Limitations of Current Solutions

Current bacterial and cell-based protein production systems present multiple challenges in terms of cost and scalability. For example, bacterial systems require continuous maintenance to ensure the healthy growth of cells. As well, bacterial platforms require the gene of interest to be transformed into the cell to express the protein if it is not native to the cell, which is not an efficient process for all strains (Cid and Jorge, 2021). And, following transformation, it is possible that the gene may undergo mutation or the bacteria may lose the transformed plasmid, which results in the need for constant validation to ensure that the correct protein is being synthesized (Silva et al, 2012). Furthermore, once verified, transitioning into integrating these systems into large-scale production pipelines and optimizing the processes can also become difficult and expensive (Hussein et al., 2021). Overall, while cellular systems can result in relatively high yields, they can quickly become costly to establish and maintain.

Due to the pressing nature of the issue, several strategies are being implemented to reduce CO2 globally. This includes transitioning to renewable energy sources, afforestation and reforestation efforts, progressive climate policies and international agreements, and carbon capture projects. Despite global advances in these carbon reduction strategies, methods such as current CO2 capture technologies still face critical limitations. Conventional carbon capture technologies, such as amine-based chemical scrubbing or direct air capture, are not readily accessible due to their high costs, slow scalability, inefficient CO2 conversion rates, and possible additional environmental challenges, including the production of toxic by-products (Leonzio and Nilay, 2024). These barriers highlight the urgent need for a more efficient, scalable, and environmentally benign alternative.

Project Problem and Solution

Climate change is a pressing issue that requires innovative solutions to address. Global warming is largely attributed to excess greenhouse gases, such as CO2, in our atmosphere (Kabir et al., 2023). As well, current cellular protein production systems can be improved to be more efficient, cost-effective, and robust enough to easily switch between different protein types. To address challenges with current solutions proposed for rising CO2 concentrations and the limitations of cellular protein production systems, one approach is to develop a reusable and scalable cell-free platform capable of producing large quantities of proteins. By synthesizing carbonic anhydrase, we aim to help alleviate rising atmospheric CO2 levels by exploiting its ability for carbon fixation.

Carbonic anhydrase is a fast enzyme in nature that converts CO2 into bicarbonate with exceptional speed and efficiency. A biomimetic approach to tackle CO2 conversion could offer a transformative path for  CO2 capture that is biodegradable and non-toxic. As well, carbonic anhydrase is able to function with low-energy costs and under mild conditions, which reduces operating costs and the need to retrofit older infrastructure, and its applications can be scaled for larger purposes (De Oliveira Maciel et al., 2022). By leveraging the biological efficiency of a carbonic anhydrase and the versatile use of DNA hydrogels, the development and integration of carbonic anhydrase-based capture systems could be an exciting alternative strategy to traditional carbon capture methods that address the pressing limitations of existing decarbonization efforts.

Our project would involve a cell-free DNA hydrogel platform, allowing for minimized costs and upkeep requirements, and could be optimized to be reusable and scalable. We would aim to have comparable or improved yields to literature DNA hydrogels and cell-based systems. Our DNA hydrogel CFFP would be designed to be flexible so that the plasmids and supplemental lysates could be easily switched to produce other proteins, allowing it to be used to fight climate change through various other applications.

Project Relevance

Protein production is a cornerstone technique in biotechnology, and can be utilized for everything from environmental engineering to drug development. Research on novel methods to express proteins is a rapidly growing field. Yet, current methods often involve trade-offs between cost, speed, and scalability. Our project introduces a cell-free, DNA hydrogel-based platform designed to overcome these barriers by providing an adaptable and rapid way to make proteins. We have chosen to express carbonic anhydrase in our project as a proof of concept because of its important role in reducing CO2, one of the main contributors to climate change. As our world rapidly approaches a critical point in the fight against climate change, there is a heightened need for continuous innovation to prevent the exacerbation of atmospheric CO2 levels. Our cell-free system demonstrates a potential method to contribute to global sustainability by efficiently producing carbonic anhydrase.

With future development, our technology can be applied in larger industrial settings to synthesize enzymes like carbonic anhydrase that are useful against various effects related to climate change. The ability to scale up enzyme production using cell lysate and DNA p-gels on an industrial level would allow for efficient expression of large amounts of enzyme to target specific aspects of climate change or other global issues. As well, the versatility of our technology extends far beyond carbonic anhydrase and can be applied to large-scale enzyme manufacturing, therapeutic protein development, and research in academic labs. For example, rapid protein expression is important in healthcare settings for the development of vaccines for novel viruses. When a new virus emerges, scientists and health care professionals need to be able to quickly express large amounts of non-infectious viral proteins for use in developing diagnostic tests and in vaccines to induce the body’s immune response. Expression of carbonic anhydrase specifically can also be useful in other situations, such as in a medical setting where isolated carbonic anhydrase could be used in treatments against conditions caused by hypercapnia (elevated CO2 levels in the blood).

We will define the success of our project based on several key outcomes. First, we aimed to confirm the presence of the plasmid within an E. coli background, which was verified by the presence of the antibiotic resistance gene. This allows for the selection of transformed colonies by growing E. coli on selective media and ensures only cells containing the plasmid will survive. Next, we will demonstrate successful crosslinking between the X-DNA and linearized plasmid, followed by reproducible gelation of the hydrogel using a PDMS mould to give it its shape. We will then isolate CA from the P-gel and perform a CA assay to confirm that the extracted protein functions similarly to the positive control provided in the assay kit. Finally, we will compare the yield of CA obtained from our system with the gold standard method of protein production, establishing benchmarks for the efficiency of our system.

Feasibility

Our design is a DNA-based hydrogel system for a cell-free protein production of Carbonic Anhydrase (CA), also called a P-Gel. Cell-free systems have been consistently used in literature to produce proteins for both scientific and preparative purposes, highlighting their effectiveness and robustness. The increased yield harvested from cell-free systems, in addition to the simplified isolation process due to the absence of whole cell proteins, makes it an effective method of production.

Although our project aims to produce CA, a protein that has not previously been made using this type of cell-free system, many individual components are well established in the literature, including formation of the P-gel, plasmid linearization, and isolation using a histidine-tagged protein. The time frame for generation and linearization of the expression plasmid can be completed in 51 hours and in parallel with the preparation of the X-DNA which should take an estimated 16 hours. Finally, preparation of the P-gel pads takes approximately 4 hours (Park et al., 2009). All parts of the project can be completed using readily available equipment and reagents, with limited user learning curves, allowing team members to easily complete required steps. Isolating the protein requires only a single column with limited troubleshooting and buffer adjustments due to the already relatively clean upstream material compared to non-cell-free systems, such as those from Escherichia coli. Once the carbonic anhydrase is produced and isolated, a simple colourimetric assay will be used to confirm the concentration of active CA, ensuring we have produced the intended product in its active form.

Challenges

One of our main challenges early on in the design of our project was to determine what protein to produce using the P-gel. Several enzymes have potential applications in climate change, however, due to its speed and relatively low biological requirements, we decided to focus on carbonic anhydrase (Chegwidden and Carter, 2000). Once we chose an enzyme, we also had to perform research into the various isoforms of carbonic anhydrase to determine which would be the most efficient and best-suited for our project. Based on our research, we chose carbonic anhydrase II due to several factors:

  1. High catalytic efficiency (Chegwidden and Carter, 2000)
  2. Relatively small size and monomeric structure (which we hypothesized would make it easier to produce)
  3. Being an extremely well-studied isoform
  4. Containing post-translational modifications that can be accomplished within Escherichia coli lysate systems (Di Fiore et al., 2020)

After determining the enzyme and specific isoform we would focus our project on, we also experienced difficulties designing the plasmid due to the many requirements the plasmid would have to meet. The plasmid would have to be compatible with our chosen isoform, and would need to incorporate elements that allow for a protein purification method that is possible for an undergraduate team to perform. Our research suggested that one possible isolation method that may work for our purposes was to use histidine tags in conjunction with TEV protease cleavage, which would require us to include the necessary sequences (Cabrita et al., 2006). We would also have to design enzyme cut sites to produce overhangs compatible with the crosslinker DNA used in the hydrogel, so that the gene could be incorporated into the hydrogel design, which also required significant consideration and research.
