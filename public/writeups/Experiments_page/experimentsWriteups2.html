P-gel Formation

Abbreviations Used

  • P-Gel: Protein-Producing Hydrogel

Introduction

After forming the PDMS molds, the protein-producing DNA hydrogel (P-gel) could be synthesized. This process involved three critical steps. First, a gel precursor solution consisting of X-DNA, plasmid, ligase, and buffer was formed.  Next, the precursor solution was placed on a glass slide and incubated, allowing for the gel to form (using DNA ligase to covalently crosslink the X-DNA and linearized plasmid components). Lastly, the P-gel pads were carefully detached from the PDMS and stored in 1x TAE buffer. Overall, this protocol took approximately 4 hours, and the steps were performed in parallel to produce multiple p-gels simultaneously (Park et al., 2009).

Techniques

Cross-Linking & DNA Ligation

DNA ligation is the process of covalently linking DNA strands by forming a phosphodiester bond between a 3′-OH and a 5′-phosphate in an ATP-dependent reaction catalyzed by DNA ligase (Park et al., 2009). In our X-DNA hydrogel, monomers first assemble by sticky-end hybridization to create a temporary and weak DNA network of nicked X-DNA and linearized plasmid junctions. Ligation then seals these nicks, converting the annealed sticky ends into continuous backbones and locking the X-DNA and plasmid network into a covalently crosslinked gel. This step increases the hydrogel’s stability and prevents dissociation under dilution. (Park et al., 2009).

Confocal Validation

Confocal microscopy is a high-resolution optical imaging technique that allows visualization of 3D structures within gels at the microscale level. In this protocol, it was used to confirm that the P-gel formed a continuous, interconnected network. To visualize DNA using confocal microscopy, intercalating fluorescent SYBR Green dyes were used. These dyes insert between the base pairs of double-stranded DNA and emit bright fluorescence when excited by a laser, effectively tracing the distribution and density of DNA strands throughout the gel. By scanning across the z-axis, the confocal microscope produced layered optical sections that revealed uniform fluorescence and a stable crosslinked matrix, validating successful hydrogel formation (Park et al., 2009).

Methods

To produce the P-gel pads, a P-gel precursor solution was prepared consisting of X-DNA stock solution, linearized plasmid solution, T4 DNA ligase, 10x DNA ligase buffer, and nuclease-free water (Park et al., 2009). The solution was prepared with an X-DNA to plasmid ratio of 3000:1 in the volumes and concentrations listed in Table 1.

To form the pads themselves, 8.0 µL of p-gel precursor solution was placed onto a glass slide and sandwiched with the fabricated PDMS mold, with gentle pressure exerted to squeeze out any excess solution. This was incubated at room temperature for at least 4 hours to allow the P-gel to form by complete crosslinking by DNA ligase in line with previous work by Park et al. (2009). After the gel was formed, the PDMS mold was peeled off, and the P-gel pads were washed off the glass slide using nuclease-free water. Pads were then gently pipetted into a 1.5 mL microcentrifuge tube and stored in a 4°C refrigerator for future addition to the lysate system.

Confocal microscopy was used to validate the formation of a P-gel. The P-gel was stained with a DNA intercalating dye, SYBR Green, to visualize its internal structure. Specifically, 10 µL of the hydrogel was incubated with the dye at room temperature for 10 minutes, allowing the fluorescent molecules to insert between double-stranded DNA regions within the network (ThermoFisher Scientific, 2022). Excess dye was removed by gentle washing with a buffer to reduce background fluorescence. The stained sample was then transferred onto a glass slide and imaged using a confocal laser scanning microscope equipped with a 488 nm excitation laser. Z-stack images were collected across the full gel depth and reconstructed into a 3D projection to assess the connectivity of the crosslinked DNA network.

Results & Discussion

Upon mixing X-DNA monomers with DNA ligase at room temperature, the solution rapidly became viscous, indicating successful initiation of crosslinking (Figure 2a). After stamping with the mold for 4 hours, a square-shaped gel pad was obtained (Figures 2b-c). Of the 10 P-gel pads prepared, only 2 formed correctly and were extractable. This low yield is likely due to inadequate vortexing of the master solution, which may have led to uneven distribution of ligase and incomplete crosslinking. These observations suggest that gel formation occurred at the macroscopic scale.

Figure 2: Formation of P-gel following ligation of X-DNA monomers. (a) Viscous solution was observed immediately after mixing X-DNA monomers with DNA ligase at room temperature, indicating initiation of crosslinking. (b-c) Mold-stamped square-shaped P-gel pads obtained after 4 hours of incubation in solution (b) or on a glass slide (c).

To confirm the P-gel’s formation and crosslinking at the microscopic level, confocal microscopy was performed as shown in Figures 3a and 3b. At the 20 μm scale (Figure 3a), a green mesh-like structure is visible, indicating successful formation of a DNA hydrogel network. Several dense clumps are also observed, likely resulting from incomplete dissociation of the lyophilized X-DNA during rehydration or from self-association of supercoiled plasmids. At higher magnification (100 μm scale, Figure 3b), the network appears as a fine, continuous mesh interspersed with bright aggregates, again consistent with partially dissociated DNA clusters. These findings confirm that crosslinking occurred and that the P-gel network formed as expected, though optimization of DNA solubilization may further improve uniformity and downstream use of the gel.

(a) A 20 μm scale image showing a green mesh-like hydrogel network with visible DNA clumps, likely due to incomplete dissociation of lyophilized X-DNA or plasmid self-association. (b) A 100 μm scale image at higher magnification reveals a finer, continuous DNA hydrogel mesh interspersed with bright aggregates corresponding to partially dissociated DNA regions.
