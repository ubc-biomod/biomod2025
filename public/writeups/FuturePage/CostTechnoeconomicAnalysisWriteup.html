Cost Analysis of Protein Production: Comparative Overview of Hydrogel-Based Cell Free Systems

Introduction

Why Is This Important?

The cost of protein production directly impacts how protein research, product development, and scalability.

In recent years[add reference], cell-free protein synthesis methods have been proposed as an efficient and rapid alternative that bypasses many bottlenecks and barriers when compared to cell-based methods. For example, it has been hypothesized to enable faster prototyping of genetic constructs for synthetic biology, supports GMP-compatible workflows for therapeutic protein production, and is ideal for low-volume, field-deployable biomanufacturing applications where speed and adaptability are essential.

Our Models:

In order to understand the economic and cost tradeoffs for cell-free protein production (CFPP) in comparison to cell-based protein production (CBPP), we chose to investigate three common use cases of protein production.

We do note that Cell-based E.Coli can reach up to expression rates of up to 10mg/mL[b], however, usually those optimizations are extremely protein-dependent and not intended for arbitrary protein. Given that there is a higher production rate in the cell-free system, from a purely production rate standpoint, CFPP has an upper hand in faster turnaround for experiments, product development, and urgent manufacturing scenarios—such as pandemic response or point-of-care diagnostics.

Final Discussion

  • Raw Materials:

Previous literature has indicated that raw material costs take up the majority of cell-free expression systems <cite technoeconomic [i]paper>. A similar pattern exists for our hydrogel-based CFPP system, as raw materials need to be purchased fresh for each new batch of expression, and cannot be regenerated like the cell-based expression. This includes all components of the p-gel and processing the DNA hydrogel to form the micropads.

Our CFPP cost breakdown shows that lysate has taken up the majority of costs, and that lysate is required to be purchased for each new batch of expression.

  • Recycling DNA (micropads)

Recycling DNA does reduce per cycle costs, as seen in Figures 1a, Figure 2a and 3a but not significantly, as DNA is not the primary contributor to the cost.

  • Switching Proteins

Previous literature has suggested that a significant use case for cell-free production systems are for the large majority of orphan drugs[j] and single use protein production required for certain projects.

With the hydrogel-based cell-free systems: the only change is a plasmid template switch, which does not change the costs.

With cell-based systems, it requires a recombinant transformation, which in our models we had set a generous 24 additional hours.

Additionally, a cost we did not consider for this use case is the requirement for stable cell-line production for each new fresh protein. For protein expressed via bacterial recombinants to be used in clinical settings, stable cell lines must be developed and certified. This often takes 12-16 months, in addition to the annual certification renewment (est $46000 annual cost for small-scale biotechs). This was time cost and resource cost that was not factored into our analysis since the 12-16 months would skew production rates to make our models incomparable with each other. This time and resource cost does significantly hinder speed-to-market, which our hydrogel-based CFPP system does not face, as it purchases the valid dna template each time.

Conclusion

In the final conclusion, we note that cell-free systems are not suited for long term expressions of one consistent protein due to the high raw material cost per cycle. However, there is a market for orphan drugs and prototype protein amounts, especially if used for prototyping in clinical settings, production here is bottle-necked by 12-16 months of waiting due to stable cell line development. This is a constraint that CFPP can easily overcome

Methods Used Cost analysis

CBPP: The cell-based protein production model is based on a high density E. coli expression workflow,  [CITE https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/pro.102 ] including transformation, batch culture, induction, and purification as seen in Figure 6. Cultures are grown in defined media with 1% glucose, reaching an OD600 of ~15 within 36 hours. Protein expression is induced at OD600 ~4–5 using 0.5 mM IPTG and incubated overnight at 20 °C. Cells are harvested at OD600 10–20, followed by lysis (e.g., high-pressure homogenization) and centrifugation to isolate the soluble protein. Typical yields range from 14-34 mg per 50 mL culture.

Figure 6.

CFPP: The cell-free protein production model is based on an E. coli extract-based transcription–translation system by Park et al (see Figure 7.). Crude lysates are prepared by lysing harvested E. coli cell paste using CelLytic reagent (10 mL per gram of wet biomass), followed by clarification steps. Cell-free reactions are assembled with lysate, energy mix, amino acids, and DNA templates. In the micropad-based system, yields of 2–3 mg/mL can be achieved over 24–36 hours per reaction cycle. Reactions are typically performed in 50 μL volumes, with approximately 400 micropads per mL of feed. Protein is recovered from the soluble reaction mixture without the need for cell lysis or complex downstream clarification. This system forms the basis for cost and yield modeling in the cell-free technoeconomic analysis.
